# Humanizer findings: Alison M. Cheperdak copy

This document flags AI-writing patterns across every section of the copy file and offers concrete suggestions. The source copy has not been altered.

---

## Executive summary

The copy is well-structured and clearly written by someone who knows the subject. The voice in the **Substack bio** is genuinely warm and personal — it reads like a real person telling her story. The **LinkedIn** and **platform bios** are professional and appropriate. The biggest concerns live in the **website pages** (Homepage, Coaching, Speaking, Book), where the writing leans heavily on patterns that read as AI-generated or AI-polished. Fixing these sections would make the whole body of copy feel more consistent with the authentic Substack voice.

## Cross-cutting issues (appearing throughout)

### 1. "Confidence, clarity, and grace" — at least 8 occurrences
This phrase appears in the LinkedIn bio, About page, Goodreads, Amazon, Evie, YouTube, Google Business, Homepage, Coaching page, and Speaking page. It has become a tic rather than a tagline. If it's the brand tagline, use it once per page maximum. If it's not, vary it.

### 2. "The small choices that shape how others feel in your presence" — at least 5 occurrences
Same issue. Powerful the first time, invisible by the fifth.

### 3. Wall-of-bold on Coaching and Book pages
Bolding every sentence is one of the most recognizable AI-output patterns. It flattens emphasis — if everything is emphasized, nothing is.

### 4. Emoji section headers on all website pages
Real copywriters and designers don't use emoji as section markers in professional website copy. These read as ChatGPT output formatting. Remove all of them.

### 5. "Not about X, it's about Y" negative parallelism — at least 6 occurrences
- "isn't about perfection—it's about knowing what to do"
- "not about memorizing rules. It's about knowing how to respond"
- "not about rigid rules or outdated expectations. It's about knowing what to say"
- "not about memorizing rules. It's about developing a presence"
- Multiple variations

This construction appears so frequently it becomes a verbal tic. Use it once, at most, per page.

### 6. Em dash density
The website pages average one em dash every 2–3 sentences. Human writing uses them sparingly. Replace 60–70% with commas, periods, or colons.

### 7. Internal notes left in copy
Lines like "FULL HOMEPAGE COPY", "HIGH-CONVERTING", "IMPORTANT—keep this", "this is where you stand out", "optimized" — these are working notes that should be stripped before the copy goes live.

## Priority recommendations

1. **Strip all emoji from section headers** on website pages. Use plain headings.
2. **Remove bold from body copy** on Coaching and Book pages. Reserve bold for headings and the occasional key phrase.
3. **Vary "confidence, clarity, and grace"** — use it once per page at most. Find 2–3 alternative phrasings.
4. **Reduce "not about X, it's about Y"** to one instance per page maximum.
5. **Cut em dashes by 60%** across website copy. Replace with commas or periods.
6. **Rewrite the Coaching page hero** in Alison's Substack voice — specific, first-person, honest rather than empathy-mirroring.
7. **Vary service description structures** on the Homepage — not every card should follow the same "[Verb] your [noun] with [positive adjective]" template.
8. **Remove all internal working notes** before publishing.
9. **Use the Substack bio as the voice reference** for all rewrites.
